If you're doing it strictly statistically, you don't have to take bluffing into account. You just need to weigh the odds and adjust the weights for each decision as the bet amount changes. But whether someone is or isn't bluffing doesn't change your odds of getting a full house.

Also, you can't have a solved game with, say, poker. (Assuming I'm understanding this concept of "solved game" correctly.) It's a game of imperfect information, you never know what your opponents are holding until the reveal. A solved game requires being able to predict an outcome based on each player's decisions, and you can only do that if you have perfect information. In chess, for instance, where nothing is hidden.

#321092 storyyeller Sun, 23rd Dec '12 5:14:00 PM from Appleloosa
Also, you can't have a solved game with, say, poker. (Assuming I'm understanding this concept of "solved game" correctly.) It's a game of imperfect information, you never know what your opponents are holding until the reveal. A solved game requires being able to predict an outcome based on each player's decisions, and you can only do that if you have perfect information. In chess, for instance, where nothing is hidden

You can still solve it theoretically, at least with two players. The problem with more than two players is that it is no longer zero sum and you have issues with stuff like kingmaker sceneraios.

Note that solving a game only means you know the optimal strategy for any possible observed situation. You may still lose, just due to bad luck, but the given strategy will maximize your expected odds over all possible strategies, assuming unbounded rational opponents. Of course the entire point of poker is that your opponents are not rational.
