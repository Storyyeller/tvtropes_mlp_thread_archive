#11375 Pykrete Fri, 30th Mar '12 5:35:52 PM from Viridian Forest
Sort of. In theory a takedown is of extremely minimal harm and easily reversible for a wrongful allegation (as opposed to, say, incarceration), so a website would show good faith in maintaining IP (and thus qualify for safe harbor) by reacting immediately. It's less guilty until proven innocent, and more stop the presses until the problem is solved.

The problem is that in practice, the presses don't start up again because the process stops there. Appealing a wrongful takedown kicks things into full-out legal mode, which the average user isn't likely to have the resources to go through even if they're in the right — which they quite often are, as something like a third of all takedown notices are outright copyright trolling. Furthermore, the sheer number of notices are such that they don't have the manpower to properly investigate that many of them. Most can't be arsed to verify that it was sent by an actual rights holder, much less poke through and make an executive decision on the nuances of fair use, etc.

The other problem is that with the whole ISP spying syndicate thing coming up in a few months, they actually have circumvented the legal process altogether and turned it into flat-out assumption of guilt.
